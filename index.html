“What drives Olympic success?”

Is it purely athletic prowess, or do deeper national and global forces shape the distribution of medals? Here, we explore the interplay between medals, economics, demographics, and history–revealing that the Olympics are as much a reflection of context as they are of competition.

We begin with population and its connection to Olympic medals. In the early Games, medal distribution was unpredictable. This moment captures a world still recovering from World War I, before modern sport systems emerged.

By 1960, the global landscape was changing. More countries are participating, and population alone still doesn’t guarantee success. This view offers a snapshot of a postwar world where Olympic competition begins to mirror rising political and social complexities.

Fast forward to the modern Games. With globalization and growing investment in sports, larger nations are expected to dominate…but do they? Here, we ask how much population matters in a world where access and infrastructure shape outcomes more than sheer numbers.

Now we turn to wealth, represented by gross domestic product (GDP). Does having more money lead to more medals? In 1920, the correlation is weak. This was a time when the Olympics were still finding their identity, and national investment in sports had yet to become a widespread strategy.
